# The Second Valley of Death — capture, transition & profit-realization playbook

The technology has already won the arguments it can win: it performed, it piloted, warfighters validated it. The instinct to reach for more demos, more champions, more relationship-building answers a question the Department already answered. This is not a **proof** problem. It is a **programming** problem.

The gap between a successful pilot and a fielded program is the **second valley of death**. Cross it by engineering the bureaucracy with the same rigor we engineer the product. It has four structural walls.

## The four walls

**1. Money.** Pilots run on flexible RDT&E / OTA / innovation-cell dollars. Scaling requires *procurement* funds programmed into the POM roughly **two years before execution**. An enthusiastic program manager cannot buy what was never programmed.
- Find the **resource sponsor** (owns the money) — not just the end user (loves the capability). Get into the POM conversation *early*, before the demo.
- Bridge the gap with mechanisms purpose-built for this valley: **APFIT**, **mid-tier acquisition (MTA)**, **software acquisition pathway (SWP)**.

**2. Requirements.** Without a *validated requirement*, nobody has the authority to scale, no matter how good the demo was. Tie the capability to a documented gap / sponsor requirement so the authority to scale exists.

**3. Contracts.** If the pilot vehicle had no production path built in, success leads back to a full **recompete**, and momentum dies in source selection.
- Begin with the transition in mind: structure pilots on vehicles with a built-in scale path — **OTAs with follow-on production authority under 10 U.S.C. 4022(f)**, or **SBIR Phase III sole-source eligibility** — so success converts directly into a contract instead of a restart.

**4. Incentives.** A PM who integrates new tech owns every schedule slip it causes and captures none of the upside. That risk aversion is **rational**, not cultural. Make "yes" cheap and career-safe:
- **Open interfaces / MOSA compliance** — extend the baseline instead of threatening it.
- **Government Purpose Rights (GPR), in writing** — so IP/data negotiations never stall adoption.
- **ATO reciprocity** — so each new program isn't a fresh accreditation fight.

## The SBIR/STTR ladder

SBIR/STTR is a transition *ladder*, not a grant. Climb it deliberately:
- **Phase I** (~$150–250K, feasibility): prove the concept and the architecture. Phase I is feasibility — a stand-alone lab and full integration are Phase II/III. Win Phase I to earn the *right* to Phase II; the money is not the prize, the position is.
- **Phase II** (~$1–2M, prototype): build the prototype and a real commercialization plan. **Direct-to-Phase-II (D2P2)** skips Phase I when prior work already shows feasibility — pursue it when you can document the readiness.
- **Phase III** (any size, *no SBIR funds*): the payoff. Work that "derives from, extends, or completes" prior SBIR work is **Phase III sole-source eligible** — a contracting officer can award it without recompete, with no dollar ceiling, for the life of the technology. This is the single most valuable contracting right a small business holds. Protect the SBIR data-rights chain that makes it possible.
- **Bridges across the Phase II→III gap**: **TACFI/STRATFI** (AFWERX matching) and **APFIT** turn a successful prototype into fielded dollars while procurement is programmed. Line up the bridge *before* Phase II ends.

## OTA / CSO / BAA mechanics

Contracts and OTAs now feed the radar alongside SBIR — each has its own scale path:
- **Other Transaction Agreements (10 U.S.C. 4022)**: prototype OTs can convert to **follow-on production under 4022(f)** *without* recompete if the prototype is completed successfully and that intent is stated up front. Always confirm 4022(f) language is in the agreement — that clause *is* the transition.
- **Consortia** (Tradewinds/AIxCC-style, NSTXL/SOSSEC/etc.): join the consortium that owns the marketplace for your domain; many OTs flow only to members. Membership is the door.
- **Commercial Solutions Openings (CSOs)**: solution-brief format (problem → solution+innovation → approach+feasibility → transition → team → cost), not a 12-section SBIR volume. Lead with the discriminator and the production path.
- **BAAs**: white-paper first; invest only after an encouraging white-paper response.

## Color of money & the PPBE clock

You cannot fix a money problem you can't name:
- **RDT&E** (6.1 basic → 6.4 adv. component dev) funds research/prototyping — flexible, where pilots live.
- **Procurement** funds buying fielded systems — rigid, multi-year, *must be programmed in the POM*.
- **O&M** funds sustainment/operations.
The **PPBE/POM** cycle programs procurement dollars ~2 years out. The whole game on the Money wall is getting a resource sponsor to program procurement (or a bridge — APFIT/MTA/SWP) before the pilot ends, not after. "Great demo, no line of funding" is the default failure mode.

## Capture before the RFP

The bid is won (or lost) before it posts:
- **Shape the requirement**: engage during **sources-sought / RFI** to influence scope toward your discriminators; respond substantively, not with marketing.
- **Use the sanctioned channels**: the SBIR topic **Q&A window** (TPOC answers on the record), **industry days / APBI**, the program-office mailbox — never cold/mass email.
- **Team early**: lock teaming agreements (for hardware: Jesse as US small-business **prime**, the **Australian partner** as subcontractor — within SBIR sub limits: ≤1/3 Phase I, ≤1/2 Phase II) before writing a word.
- **Find the resource sponsor before the demo.** The end user opens the door; the resource sponsor owns the money.

## Proposal craft

- **Compliance is pass/fail.** Build a compliance matrix mapping every "shall/must" to the exact section that answers it. Evaluators score what they can find.
- **Win themes vs discriminators**: a win theme is the one-sentence reason you win; discriminators are the specific, provable facts (real metrics, TRL, prior awards) that no generic bidder can claim. Ground every claim — cite real numbers, never invent.
- **Ghost the incumbents**: name the gaps in the current state of the art (and the award incumbents) without naming names, so the evaluator reads your solution as the fix.
- **Write to the evaluator's lens**: feasibility for Phase I; transition realism for Phase II/OTA. Past performance + CPARS build the credibility that wins the next one.

## Allied / AUKUS pathway

For hardware, Jesse's **Australian partner builds and funds** (especially USVs); Jesse leads software + design as US prime.
- **AUKUS Pillar II** (advanced capabilities — maritime autonomy, AI, cyber) is an explicit US–AUS cooperation lane and a genuine tailwind for allied teaming.
- **Guardrails**: shared technical data is **ITAR/EAR** export-controlled; foreign nationals cannot access US **classified/CUI** — keep the partner on unclassified, exportable hardware and structure Jesse's US-cleared side accordingly. Treat these as structuring steps, not blockers; prefer AUKUS-authorized arrangements.

## The plays

1. **Begin with the transition in mind.** Pick the pilot vehicle for its scale path (4022(f) follow-on production, SBIR Phase III), not just for ease of award.
2. **Find the resource sponsor before the demo.** Get into the POM early; name the line of funding.
3. **Cut the integration tax structurally.** MOSA + GPR-in-writing + ATO reciprocity make the PM's "yes" cheap and career-safe.
4. **Borrow transition infrastructure.** Ride proven rails — established ATOs, standing program-office relationships, consortium memberships, existing vehicles — instead of building from zero.
5. **Protect the Phase III chain.** Every SBIR deliverable preserves sole-source eligibility for the life of the technology; never sign it away.

## Proof it works

Modern Day Marine 2026 AI Forum hackathon: an all-Marine team took **first place with SPIRE** (contested-logistics decision support). Before celebrating, they confirmed **Government Purpose Rights in writing** — so IP could never become the reason a program office said no. The demo opens the door; the paperwork lets the capability walk through.

## How to use this in the workspace

Each pursuit is scored on the four walls (Money / Requirements / Contracts / Incentives). The weakest wall is the next thing to engineer — not the next demo. The lifecycle runs bid → award → pilot → **transition** → POM → program of record → revenue, and the goal of every interaction is to make adoption the path of least resistance.
